\section{Spin-Boson Model}
\label{subsec:qubit_exact_geometry}

We now put the machinery of Secs.~\ref{sec:generating_function}--\ref{sec:qubit_closure} to work
in the prototypical case of an open quantum system - the Spin-Boson model. This couples a single spin to the environment, and its $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra is rich enough to generate non-trivial corrections, while being simple enough to yield easily tractable results. Critically however, this latter feature is \emph{convenient} for our purposes, and not reflective of a limitation of the general framework. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:bloch_overview}, the exact qubit mean-force state $\mathbf{r}$ lives in a three-dimensional Bloch sphere, but is geometrically locked to the \emph{coupling plane} (shaded) spanned by the system axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$ and the interaction axis $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$. The orientation of this plane is defined by the interaction phase $\phi$, which directly determines the phase of the induced coherences in the reduced state. 

We first define our system Hamiltonian and coupling via
\begin{equation}
    H_Q = \frac{\omega_q}{2} \hat{\mathbf{n}}_s \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \qquad f = \hat{\mathbf{f}} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$ and $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$ are unit vectors defining the system and coupling axes, respectively.
 The overall coupling strength $g$ is omitted in the following derivation for clarity and can be re-introduced as a global scaling factor where needed.
For the derivation it is convenient to work in the eigenbasis of $H_Q$ (so
$\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s=\hat{\mathbf z}$). The final formulas are then rewritten
covariantly in terms of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$ and $\hat{\mathbf{f}}_\perp$.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering

    \caption{3D geometric overview of the qubit mean-force state. The bare system is defined by the axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$ at the north pole, with the bare thermal state vector pointing towards the south pole with length $r(\beta)$. The interaction axis $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$ is also shown. The exact reduced state $\mathbf{r}$ has magnitude $r(\tilde{\beta})$ and is tilted by the effective angle $\tilde{\varphi}$ relative to the bare state.     \label{fig:bloch_overview}
\end{figure}



The influence exponent may easily be calculated directly, using $\tilde f(\tau)=e^{\tau H_Q} f e^{-\tau H_Q}$. In the qubit energy basis ($\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s = \hat{\mathbf{z}}$), we decompose the unit coupling vector as $\hat{\mathbf{f}} = (s \cos\phi, s \sin\phi, c)$, where $c \equiv \cos\theta$ and $s \equiv \sin\theta$ denote the longitudinal and transverse projections relative to the system axis, and $\phi$ is the azimuthal angle. Writing
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    f &= c\sigma_z + f_-\sigma_+ + f_+\sigma_-, \\
    f_\pm &\equiv s e^{\pm i \phi},
\end{split}
\end{equation}
gives
\begin{equation}
    \tilde f(\tau)=c\sigma_z+f_-e^{\omega_q\tau}\sigma_+ + f_+e^{-\omega_q\tau}\sigma_-,
\end{equation}
meaning $\Delta(\beta)$ is determined solely by the bath response kernels at the frequencies $0,\pm\omega_q$. Consequently,
all bath dependence in $\Delta$ is compressed to scalar kernel data evaluated
at the same frequencies. Using the notation of
Sec.~\ref{subsec:kms_symmetry}, the required bath objects are
$\mathcal{K}(0)$, $\mathcal{K}(\pm\omega_q)$, and $R(0)$,
$R(\pm\omega_q)$. 

For a single qubit, the operator algebra closes almost trivially. Using $\sigma_+\sigma_-=(\mathbb
I+\sigma_z)/2$, $\sigma_-\sigma_+=(\mathbb I-\sigma_z)/2$, and
$\sigma_\pm^2=0$, one finds
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \tilde{f}(\tau)\tilde{f}(\tau')
    &= \Big[c^2 + s^2\cosh\big(\omega_q(\tau-\tau')\big)\Big]\mathbb{I} \\
    &\quad + s^2\sinh\big(\omega_q(\tau-\tau')\big)\sigma_z \\
    &\quad + c f_- \left(e^{\omega_q\tau'} - e^{\omega_q\tau}\right)\sigma_+ \\
    &\quad + c f_+ \left(e^{-\omega_q\tau} - e^{-\omega_q\tau'}\right)\sigma_- .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Using the response kernels, the entirety of the bath's influence is compressed into two real, temperature-dependent response kernels: a longitudinal (resonant) kernel $\Sigma_z$ and a transverse (non-resonant) kernel $\Sigma_{\perp}$, defined as
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
    \Sigma_z & \equiv \frac{1}{2} \left[R(\omega_q) - R(-\omega_q)\right], \\
    \Sigma_\perp &\equiv \frac{4}{\omega_q} \left[ \cosh\left(\frac{\beta\omega_q}{2}\right) \mathcal{K}(0) - e^{-\beta\omega_q/2} \mathcal{K}(\omega_q) \right].
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:bath_kernels_direct}
\end{equation}
To expose the coupling dependence cleanly, we reintroduce an explicit
dimensionless strength $g$ through $K(u) = g^2 K_0(u)$, so that all response
kernels scale as $g^2$. In the interests of brevity, we shall subsume this factor into the definition of parameters, except in those cases where physically relevant effects depend on it. 

The reduced system's $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra means both the influence exponent and the final reduced state may be calculated exactly. After some algebraic gymnastics (done without harness in App~\ref{app:qubit_ladder_derivation}), the exact reduced density matrix $\rho_Q$ takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_Q = \frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 
        1 + m_z & m_\perp e^{-i\phi} \\
        m_\perp e^{i\phi} & 1 - m_z
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{eq:rho_matrix_general}
\end{equation}
Both the definition and the interpretation of the Bloch vector component magnetisations $(m_z, m_\perp)$ relies on several intermediate parametrisations. First and most importantly, we have the \emph{influence magnitude}
\begin{equation}
    \chi = s \sqrt{c^2 \Sigma_\perp^2 + s^2 \Sigma_z^2},
    \label{eq:chi_factored}
\end{equation}
which - as we will see shortly - acts as the essential determinant for the different regimes of behaviour for the system. It is equivalent to the \emph{norm} of the influence exponent $\|\Delta(\beta)\|^2 \equiv \chi^2$, up to the sign of $\sin(\theta)$. This sign has significant consequences, but like $g$ we shall work under the assumption $\sin(\theta)>0$ except for those cases where this sign is relevant. The reduced state's Bloch vector radius $r$ is then given by 
\begin{equation}
r = \left|\tanh\chi\right| =\sqrt{m_z^2+m_\perp^2}.
\end{equation}
If we define the parameters
\begin{align}
    \Theta &\equiv \operatorname{arctanh}(\gamma s^2 \Sigma_z) - \frac{\beta\omega_q}{2}, \label{eq:theta_param} \\
    \tan\eta &\equiv - \cot\theta \frac{\Sigma_\perp}{\Sigma_z}, \label{eq:eta_param} \\
    \gamma &= \tanh\chi / \chi, \label{eq:gamma_param}
\end{align}
then the Bloch vector components are expressed via
\begin{align}
    m_z &= \tanh\Theta, \label{eq:m_z_solution} \\
    m_\perp &= \tan\eta \left( m_z \cosh\frac{\beta\omega_q}{2} + \sinh\frac{\beta\omega_q}{2} \right), \label{eq:bloch_locking_linear} \\
    \tan\varphi &= m_\perp / m_z. \label{eq:tilt_angle_def}
\end{align}
From this, we see that the reduced state is confined to the common plane of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$ and $\hat{\mathbf{f}}$, with its radius given by $r$ and a \emph{tilt-angle} $\varphi$ away from the bare system's axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_s$. The parameter $\tan\eta$ then encodes the anisotropy of the bath's influence as a function of $\theta$.  In particular, when $\theta<\pi/2$, $\tan\eta<0$ and the state tilts toward the ground-state hemisphere. for $\theta>\pi/2$, the sign of $\tan\eta$ reverses and the state is dragged
toward the \emph{excited}-state hemisphere, corresponding to a bath-induced population
inversion relative to the bare Hamiltonian eigenbasis.  The special cases $\cos(\theta)=0$
(purely transverse coupling) has $\tan\eta=0$ identically.

The importance of this anisotropy is made clear when we consider the interpretation of $\Theta$, which serves as an effective \emph{renormalisation} of temperature. We may justify this interpretation by first noting the special case of a purely transverse coupling $\theta =\frac{\pi}{2}$. In this case the influence operator aligns with $\hat{n}_s$ the reduced system is simply a copy of the bare system with $\frac{\beta\omega_q}{2} \to \Theta$. This immediately implies that we may describe the system via an \emph{effective} temperature $\tilde{\beta}$:
\begin{equation}
\beta \to \tilde{\beta}= \frac{2\Theta}{\omega_q}.
\end{equation} 
This temperature renormalisation is a universal feature holding at any coupling angle $\theta$. Critically though, whether this renormalisation maps to a higher or lower temperature depends on the \emph{relative sign} of $\Theta$ and $\sin(\theta)$. Switching the sign of either corresponds to $m_z\to-m_z$ and population inversion.  The only contribution of the coupling angle beyond this is simply to \emph{rotate} the temperature renormalised thermal state relative to the bare orientation. We can see this most clearly by returning to Eq.\eqref{eq:bloch_locking_linear}. Defining the factor 
\begin{equation}
\kappa =\sinh(\mathrm{arctanh}(\gamma s^2 \Sigma_z)),
\end{equation}
after rearrangement, $m_\perp$ takes the form
\begin{equation}
m_\perp = -\kappa\frac{\Sigma_\perp}{\Sigma_z}\tan(\theta-\frac{\pi}{2})\cosh^{-1}\left(-\frac{\tilde{\beta}\omega_q}{2}\right).
\end{equation}
This formulation provides a natural definition for the effective longitudinal and transverse magnetisations of the dressed qubit:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{m}_{z} \equiv \frac{\Sigma_z \sinh\left(-\frac{\tilde{\beta}\omega_q}{2}\right)}{\tilde{r}}, \qquad \tilde{m}_{\perp} \equiv \frac{\kappa \Sigma_\perp}{\tilde{r}}.
    \label{eq:effective_magnetisations}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{r}(\beta)=r(\tilde{\beta})$ is the renormalised radius. This means the renormalised angle at $\tilde{\beta}$ is given by
\begin{equation}
\tan\tilde{\varphi} = \frac{\kappa \Sigma_\perp \tan(\theta-\frac{\pi}{2})}{\Sigma_z\sinh\frac{\tilde{\beta}\omega_q}{2}}.
\end{equation}
In this representation, the effective tilt angle $\tilde{\varphi}$ reflects the competition between the resonant reinforcement of the ground state and the non-resonant generation of coherence. A non-zero $\tilde{\varphi}$ signifies that the bath's influence has rotated the reduced state away from the bare energy basis, endowing the equilibrium state with apparent ``vacuum-induced'' coherences with magnitude $|\tilde{m}_\perp|$.  For $\theta>\pi/2$ the tilt is toward the excited-state direction, so the bath-induced rotation and any strong-coupling purity enhancement act together to favour excited-state population: the reduced state can be effectively population-inverted.
